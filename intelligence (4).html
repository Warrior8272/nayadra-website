Intelligence Discipline · 02 · SOCMINT

How Social Media Becomes Maritime Intelligence

By NA.YADRA Pacific Maritime Intelligence Platform

What is SOCMINT?

Social Media Intelligence — SOCMINT — is the collection and analysis of information drawn from social media platforms to produce actionable intelligence. What began as a tool for law enforcement and counterterrorism has quietly become one of the most powerful open-source disciplines available to maritime analysts today.

Unlike traditional intelligence methods that rely on sensors, satellites, or intercepted communications, SOCMINT exploits something far more human — the instinct to share. Vessel crews post harbour arrivals on Facebook. Captains photograph cargo on Instagram. Port workers check in on TikTok. Boat owners announce departures in WhatsApp groups. Every post, tag, like, and location pin is a data point.

"The ocean does not broadcast. But the people on it do."

Why It Matters in the Pacific

The Pacific Ocean presents unique challenges for maritime domain awareness. Vast distances, limited coast guard coverage, and sparse radar infrastructure mean that conventional monitoring often fails to detect vessels operating in grey areas — fishing illegally, transiting without declaration, or moving cargo that never appears on a manifest.

AIS transponders can be switched off. Radar has range limits. But social media never sleeps.

In the Pacific island context, maritime communities are tightly networked and highly active on social platforms. Fishing crews share catches. Traders announce arrivals. Village networks discuss vessel movements in real time. For a trained SOCMINT analyst, this is a continuous, unfiltered feed of maritime activity that no sensor array can replicate.

How NA.YADRA Applies SOCMINT

NA.YADRA integrates SOCMINT into its broader multi-source intelligence picture alongside AIS tracking, satellite imagery analysis, and behavioural pattern recognition. In practice this means:

Cross-referencing vessel movements — when a vessel's AIS track shows a gap or anomaly, social media can confirm or contradict the official position. A crew member's post geotagged at a port the vessel was not supposed to visit is corroborating evidence of a false track.

Monitoring vessel-associated accounts — owners, operators, crew, and agents connected to vessels of interest often maintain public social media profiles. Changes in behaviour, new connections, or unusual posting patterns can signal a change in intent or operation.

Arabic-language monitoring — a significant proportion of vessels operating in the Indo-Pacific are owned or crewed by individuals from Gulf states, with social media activity conducted entirely in Arabic. NA.YADRA's Arabic language capability allows direct monitoring of these communities without reliance on automated translation, which frequently misses context, slang, and maritime terminology.

Port and harbour community monitoring — local fishing and trading communities around Fiji's ports maintain active group chats and public pages. Information shared within these networks often reaches NA.YADRA before it reaches any official channel.

The Legal and Ethical Framework

SOCMINT conducted by NA.YADRA is limited strictly to publicly available information. No private accounts are accessed, no accounts are impersonated, and no platform terms of service are violated. This places NA.YADRA's SOCMINT activity firmly within the legal framework governing open-source intelligence collection in Fiji and internationally.

All SOCMINT-derived intelligence is clearly sourced and caveated when passed to partner agencies, ensuring that Fiji Customs and Fiji Navy can assess the reliability and origin of the information before acting on it.

SOCMINT in Action — A Pacific Example

Consider a fishing vessel that departs a known port, switches off its AIS transponder mid-transit, and reappears several hours later claiming to have been fishing. Conventional monitoring sees only the gap. SOCMINT may reveal a crew member posting photographs from a location inconsistent with the declared fishing grounds, or a contact at a remote anchorage announcing an unexpected visitor. Combined with satellite imagery showing a vessel at anchor during the dark period, the intelligence picture becomes significantly clearer.

This is the power of fusing SOCMINT with IMINT, AIS analysis, and behavioural pattern recognition — no single source tells the whole story, but together they leave very little room for ambiguity.

Intelligence Discipline · 03 · IMINT

How Satellite Imagery Exposes What AIS Cannot

By NA.YADRA Pacific Maritime Intelligence Platform

What is IMINT?

Imagery Intelligence — IMINT — is the collection and analysis of visual information captured from aerial or satellite platforms to produce actionable intelligence. Once the exclusive domain of national intelligence agencies and military commands, advances in commercial satellite technology have made IMINT accessible to specialised maritime intelligence platforms like NA.YADRA.

Where traditional maritime monitoring relies on vessels choosing to broadcast their position, IMINT asks a fundamentally different question — not where is a vessel telling us it is, but where can we actually see it?

"A vessel may switch off its transponder. It cannot hide from a camera in orbit."

The AIS Blind Spot

The Automatic Identification System was designed for collision avoidance and vessel tracking in busy shipping lanes. It was never designed to be tamper-proof. A vessel can switch off its AIS transponder in seconds, disappearing entirely from conventional tracking systems. It can broadcast false position data, spoofing its location hundreds of miles from where it actually sits. It can manipulate speed, course, and identity with minimal technical knowledge.

In the Pacific, where enforcement assets are stretched thin across millions of square kilometres of ocean, AIS manipulation is a known tactic among vessels engaged in illegal fishing, smuggling, and undeclared transits. A vessel that goes dark on AIS does not cease to exist — it simply becomes invisible to systems that rely solely on transponder data. Satellite imagery makes it visible again.

How NA.YADRA Uses IMINT

Dark vessel detection — when NA.YADRA's behavioural analysis flags an AIS gap, satellite imagery can confirm whether a vessel is physically present at the expected location or has moved to an undeclared position.

Ghost vessel correlation — vessels detected in satellite imagery that do not correspond to any AIS-broadcasting contact are flagged as ghost vessels. NA.YADRA's computer vision pipeline, built on the YOLO object detection architecture, automatically identifies vessel-shaped objects in imagery and cross-references them against the live AIS picture.

Port and anchorage monitoring — satellite imagery of known anchorages, fishing aggregation areas, and unofficial landing points around Fiji's island groups provides a ground truth check against declared vessel movements.

Change detection — repeated imagery passes over the same location allow NA.YADRA to detect changes in vessel presence, cargo handling activity, or infrastructure use that may indicate unreported operations.

Computer Vision and AI in IMINT

NA.YADRA's IMINT capability is augmented by artificial intelligence. Rather than relying on manual image analysis — time-consuming and resource-intensive at Pacific scale — NA.YADRA employs automated object detection models trained to identify vessels, estimate their size and type, and flag contacts of interest for human analyst review.

This human-machine teaming approach allows a small analytical team to process imagery coverage across Fiji's entire EEZ — nearly 1.3 million square kilometres — at a tempo that manual analysis could never sustain. The machine finds the contacts. The human analyst assesses them.

Fusing IMINT With Other Intelligence Disciplines

IMINT is most powerful when fused with other sources. A satellite image showing a vessel at anchor tells you where it is. AIS data tells you where it claimed to be. SOCMINT may reveal a crew member posting from that location. Behavioural analysis tells you whether the pattern is consistent with legitimate activity or something else entirely.

This multi-source fusion is the core of NA.YADRA's analytical approach. No single intelligence discipline is sufficient on its own. Together they build a picture that is significantly harder for a vessel of interest to deceive.

The Pacific IMINT Challenge

Cloud cover, vast distances, and the cost of commercial satellite tasking present real challenges for IMINT in the Pacific. NA.YADRA addresses this through a combination of free and commercial imagery sources, prioritised tasking based on AIS anomaly alerts, and integration with open satellite archives that provide historical imagery for pattern analysis.

When a vessel of interest is identified — such as a vessel approaching Fiji's northern EEZ with no declared destination — NA.YADRA can task available imagery resources to that vessel's projected track, providing a visual confirmation layer that AIS data alone cannot offer.

Intelligence Discipline · 04 · CULTINT

Why Culture is the Intelligence Discipline Nobody Talks About

By NA.YADRA Pacific Maritime Intelligence Platform

What is CULTINT?

Cultural Intelligence — CULTINT — is the collection, analysis, and application of cultural knowledge to produce actionable intelligence. It is the understanding that human behaviour does not occur in a vacuum. Every vessel movement, every trading pattern, every decision made at sea is shaped by the cultural context of the people making it — their language, their community ties, their traditions, their loyalties, and their fears.

Where OSINT reads data and IMINT reads imagery, CULTINT reads people. And in the Pacific, where maritime activity is inseparable from community, identity, and tradition, it is often the most important discipline of all.

The Gap in Maritime Intelligence

Most maritime intelligence platforms treat the ocean as a technical problem. They count vessels, measure speeds, calculate courses. They ask where and when. CULTINT asks why.

Why does a vessel anchor at a particular island rather than proceeding to port? Why does a fishing crew from one island group never interact with vessels from another? Why does cargo move through an informal network of inter-island traders rather than through a registered shipping company? Why does a vessel's declared route make no cultural or commercial sense for the community it claims to serve?

These questions cannot be answered by an AIS feed or a satellite image. They require cultural knowledge — an understanding of the human terrain that underlies every maritime pattern NA.YADRA tracks.

"Where OSINT reads data and IMINT reads imagery, CULTINT reads people."

The Pacific Cultural Context

The Pacific Ocean is not empty space between islands. It is a web of relationships — kinship networks, trading partnerships, political alliances, and historical rivalries — that have governed movement across the ocean for centuries before any AIS transponder existed.

In Fiji, inter-island movement follows patterns that predate modern navigation. Certain communities have traditional trading relationships with specific island groups. Certain routes carry cultural significance that shapes when and how vessels travel them. Certain anchorages are used by particular communities for reasons that have nothing to do with harbour infrastructure and everything to do with belonging.

Understanding these patterns does not just contextualise intelligence — it generates it. A vessel anchoring at a location that carries no commercial logic but deep cultural significance is not behaving randomly. It is communicating something. A trained CULTINT analyst can read that communication.

NA.YADRA's Fijian cultural foundation — embedded in the platform's name itself, drawn from the Fijian word Yadra, meaning alertness and wakefulness — is not incidental. It reflects a deliberate commitment to grounding maritime intelligence in the cultural reality of the Pacific, not imposing an external analytical framework onto it.

Arabic Language and Gulf Maritime CULTINT

The Indo-Pacific maritime space is not bounded by Pacific island communities alone. A significant and growing proportion of vessel ownership, crew supply, and cargo financing in the broader region originates from Gulf states — the UAE, Oman, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia — where maritime commerce is conducted in Arabic and within cultural frameworks that differ substantially from Western or Pacific norms.

NA.YADRA's Arabic language capability provides a direct window into this community that automated translation cannot replicate. Maritime Arabic is rich with terminology, idiom, and contextual meaning that machine translation consistently misreads. An ownership dispute that looks like a commercial disagreement in translation may, in context, reflect a specific tribal or family dimension that explains vessel behaviour in ways no database record can capture.

In practical terms this means NA.YADRA can:

Read primary source documentation — vessel registration records, port authority filings, ownership transfers, and crew manifests filed in Arabic are accessible directly, without reliance on intermediary translation that introduces delay and error.

Monitor Arabic-language social media and maritime forums — Gulf vessel owners, operators, and crew maintain active communities online where operational information, port intelligence, and vessel movements are discussed openly in Arabic. Direct monitoring surfaces intelligence that English-language monitoring entirely misses.

Contextualise Gulf vessel behaviour — understanding the cultural norms governing commercial relationships, debt, honour, and obligation in Gulf maritime communities allows NA.YADRA to interpret vessel behaviour patterns that would otherwise appear anomalous or inexplicable.

CULTINT and Community Intelligence Networks

In the Pacific island context, the most valuable CULTINT asset is not a database or a satellite feed — it is the community itself. Island communities have always been the primary observers of maritime activity in their waters. Fishermen notice unfamiliar vessels. Harbour workers observe unusual cargo. Village networks track arrivals and departures with a granularity that no sensor network can match.

NA.YADRA's approach to CULTINT recognises this explicitly. Building trusted relationships with Pacific island communities — grounded in cultural respect, mutual benefit, and a genuine understanding of local priorities — creates an intelligence network that amplifies every technical capability the platform possesses.

A dark vessel gap on AIS becomes significantly more actionable when a community observer can place an unidentified vessel at a specific anchorage during the same period. A GPS anomaly becomes clearer when local knowledge confirms that the vessel in question had no legitimate reason to be in that area. Cultural intelligence does not replace technical intelligence — it grounds it in reality.

"Cultural intelligence does not replace technical intelligence — it grounds it in reality."

The Cultural Advisor Dimension

NA.YADRA's CULTINT capability is underpinned by formal cultural advisor training — a discipline developed within military intelligence environments to enable analysts to operate effectively across cultural boundaries without the misreading, projection, or assumption that degrades intelligence quality when cultural context is ignored.

Cultural advisor training teaches analysts to suspend their own cultural framework when assessing behaviour observed in a different cultural context. It teaches the discipline of asking what this behaviour means within its own cultural logic before asking what it means within yours. Applied to maritime intelligence, this discipline is the difference between an analyst who sees an anomaly and an analyst who understands what the anomaly is communicating.

In an intelligence environment where the vessels of interest, their crews, their owners, and their communities span Pacific island, Melanesian, Polynesian, Micronesian, South and Southeast Asian, and Arab cultural contexts, this capability is not optional. It is foundational.

Conclusion

The ocean is not a neutral space. It is traversed by people, shaped by communities, and governed by cultural logics that predate every technology used to monitor it. An intelligence platform that ignores this is not seeing the full picture — it is seeing a technical abstraction of a human reality.

CULTINT is what closes the gap between data and understanding. It is what allows NA.YADRA to ask not just where a vessel is, but why it is there — and what that means for the security of Fiji's waters and the Pacific communities that depend on them.

Case Study · CASE-DRIFT-0001 · Lau Group

Operation Ogea — Vessel Behaviour Analysis and Drift Correlation in the Lau Group

By NA.YADRA Pacific Maritime Intelligence Platform | Case Reference: CASE-DRIFT-0001

Background

In late May 2026, media reporting indicated that suspected drug packages had been discovered in Fiji's Lau Group — packages found both floating at sea and washing ashore in the vicinity of Ogea and Moce islands. Fiji authorities confirmed investigations were underway.

NA.YADRA's retrospective vessel analysis identified that a vessel of interest — the motor yacht EXPLORA (MMSI: 538071191 / IMO: 1007342) — had been operating in the wider Ogea area during the relevant period. This case study documents the analytical process NA.YADRA applied to assess the significance of that presence, and the intelligence conclusions reached.

It is also a case study in analytical discipline — in what it means to follow the evidence rather than the assumption.

The Vessel

EXPLORA is a 44-metre motor yacht flying a Marshall Islands flag of convenience — a flag state commonly associated with vessels seeking minimal regulatory oversight. She departed Nadi on 05 May 2026 declaring destination "FJ CRUISING" and conducted an extended multi-island voyage through Fiji's outer island groups. MMSI: 538071191 · IMO: 1007342 · GT: 419 · Draft: 2.9m · Built: 1985.

The Voyage Timeline

AIS Loss — West of Denarau (23–24 May): EXPLORA's AIS signal was lost west of Denarau at 10.6 knots on course 293°. Assessed as a signal gap rather than confirmed deliberate deactivation, but flagged for monitoring.

Nananu-i-Ra Island (24 May): Entered the bay, conducted multiple manoeuvres, remained consistent with anchoring and recreational activity.

South Vanua Levu (24–25 May): Anchored in a sheltered bay near a settlement and jetty. Pattern consistent with a shore visit.

NW of Daliconi, Vanua Balavu (25 May): Deviated from direct route and entered a lagoon area north-west of Daliconi — a known Lau Group anchorage — remaining in protected waters with other vessels present.

Southern Ogea Waters (25–26 May): Confirmed anchored at 19°8'59.99"S / 176°25'41.88"W. Remained stationary for an extended period. During the same window, a second vessel — EARENDIL II (MMSI: 367763150, US-flagged pleasure craft) — was observed loitering in the same area with extensive localised movement over several hours.

Departure Ogea → Kadavu (30 May): Manoeuvred then departed heading west/south-west toward central Fiji waters on the Kadavu corridor, course 344.9°.

"The instinct to connect correlating facts is natural — and in intelligence, it is also one of the most dangerous analytical errors an analyst can make."

The Intelligence Trigger

The discovery of suspected drug packages in the Ogea and Moce area created a retrospective intelligence question: which vessels were operating in that area during the relevant window? NA.YADRA's behavioural analysis had already flagged EXPLORA's Ogea presence — the looping, localised AIS track around the southern Ogea waters was visually distinctive, with repeated course alterations and extended stationary periods inconsistent with a vessel simply passing through.

Drift Analysis

To assess whether EXPLORA's position was spatially consistent with the reported package discovery location, NA.YADRA conducted an illustrative backwards drift model. With wind at 270° / 12 knots and current at 250° / 1.2 knots, the model estimated a probable release area of approximately 17.65°S / 177.87°E with a probability radius of 12 nautical miles over a 24–36 hour backtrack window.

EXPLORA's confirmed anchor position was 17.705°S / 177.145°E — approximately 40–45 nautical miles west of the estimated drift origin. The separation was significant. Geographic correlation existed only in the broader sense that both positions fell within the wider Lau Group operational area.

The Analytical Picture

NA.YADRA assessed the indicators as follows: Presence in area — YES. Ogea-area activity — YES. Loitering pattern — POSSIBLE. Direct package link — NOT ESTABLISHED. AIS spoofing evidence — NONE. Physical evidence — NONE. Witness evidence — NONE. Correlation strength — LOW.

The presence of EARENDIL II loitering in the same area during the same period was noted as a parallel interest without sufficient information to assess significance independently.

The Importance of Analytical Discipline

This case illustrates one of the most important principles in intelligence analysis: correlation is not causation, and presence is not evidence.

EXPLORA was in the Ogea area. Drug packages were found in the Ogea area. NA.YADRA's assessment process explicitly tested the hypothesis against the evidence. The drift model placed the estimated release point 40–45 nautical miles from EXPLORA's confirmed position — too large a separation to sustain the correlation. EXPLORA's overall voyage pattern was consistent with a yacht cruising itinerary. No AIS anomalies, spoofing, or identity manipulation were detected. No physical, witness, or signals intelligence corroborated a direct link.

Overall assessment: Routine yacht cruising pattern. Confidence: Moderate.

"An intelligence platform that finds connections where none exist is not protecting Fiji's waters. It is generating noise."

What This Case Demonstrates

The EXPLORA/Ogea analysis showcases NA.YADRA's methodology at its most rigorous — not when it finds something, but when it honestly concludes that the evidence does not support the initial intelligence question. NA.YADRA's value to Fiji Customs and Fiji Navy is not simply in detecting anomalies. It is in providing the analytical framework to assess those anomalies correctly — to distinguish between a vessel that is present and a vessel that is responsible, between a pattern that is unusual and a pattern that is illegal, between correlation and evidence.

In the Ogea case, the evidence said: look elsewhere. That conclusion took the same rigour to reach as any other.

Analytical Ratings — Spatial Correlation: Medium · Behavioural Interest: Medium · Evidential Strength: Low · Operational Priority: Review · Confidence: Low–Medium. This case remains open for update should additional intelligence become available.

Case Study · NA-EEZ-20260701-001 · Northern EEZ

Operation Northern Watch — Real-Time EEZ Intercept, WIND SHIFT

By NA.YADRA Pacific Maritime Intelligence Platform | Case Reference: NA-EEZ-20260701-001

Background

In the early hours of 01 July 2026, NA.YADRA detected a British-flagged sailing vessel transiting toward Fiji's northern Exclusive Economic Zone from Wallis and Futuna with no declared destination. What followed was a real-time intelligence operation — from initial detection through bounding box expansion, vessel seeding, automated EEZ alert generation, formal intelligence report production, and notification to Fiji Customs — conducted entirely within the NA.YADRA platform.

This is the first live EEZ intercept recorded by NA.YADRA under operational conditions.

The Vessel

WIND SHIFT is a 20-metre British-flagged sailing vessel that departed Sigave — the main settlement of Alo, Wallis and Futuna — on 29 June 2026. MMSI: 233763000 · Flag: United Kingdom · Size: 20m / 6m beam · Last Port: Sigave, Wallis & Futuna · Departed: 29 June 2026 00:21 UTC · Speed: 8.3 knots · Course: 218.9° Southwest · AIS Destination: NOT BROADCAST.

Wallis and Futuna is a French collectivity with limited outbound customs screening, sitting approximately 480 nautical miles northeast of Fiji's northern EEZ boundary.

Phase 1 — The Coverage Gap

The first intelligence finding in this operation was not about WIND SHIFT. It was about NA.YADRA itself.

An initial database query returned no results. A bounding box audit revealed the cause: NA.YADRA's AISStream subscription covered only 176.5°E–178.5°E / 18.5°S–16.5°S — a box centred on the Suva corridor that entirely excluded the northern EEZ approach from Wallis and Futuna. Every vessel transiting the northern corridor was invisible to the platform.

This matters beyond this single vessel. The northern EEZ approach is the least monitored maritime corridor to Fiji — outside primary Fiji Navy patrol range, outside the established Customs monitoring zone, and outside the coverage of most AIS monitoring systems. It is the approach a vessel seeking to avoid detection would use.

NA.YADRA's bounding box was immediately expanded to 174.0°E–180.0°E / 10.0°S–22.0°S, adding full coverage of the northern approach corridor, Wallis and Futuna, the Lau Group eastern approaches, and the western approaches from Vanuatu.

"The most significant finding was not WIND SHIFT — it was the coverage gap that had left Fiji's northern EEZ unmonitored."

Phase 2 — Vessel Identification

With AISStream unable to capture a vessel already in transit, NA.YADRA turned to open-source vessel tracking. VesselFinder confirmed as of 01 July 2026 00:18 UTC: position inside Fiji's northern EEZ tracking toward Vanua Levu, speed 8.3 knots, course 218.9°, AIS destination field blank. At that speed and course, WIND SHIFT's track projected directly toward Vanua Levu's northern coastline with an estimated landfall window of 12–18 hours. The vessel had already crossed the boundary. The intercept window was open.

Phase 3 — EEZ Monitor Activation

NA.YADRA's EEZ monitoring system operates as a continuous background process, querying the vessel database every 60 seconds and flagging any vessel whose latitude exceeds the northern boundary threshold of -16.5°S. WIND SHIFT was seeded into the database with her confirmed position, speed, course, and full source metadata. Within one monitoring cycle the alert fired:

[EEZ ALERT] ⚠ WIND SHIFT (233763000) entered northern EEZ — lat:-15.8 lon:178.2 course:218.9°

The alert was written to data/eez_alerts.json with status UNACKNOWLEDGED and served to the NA.YADRA dashboard via /api/eez/alerts. The vessel was tracked, flagged, and visible.

Phase 4 — Intelligence Assessment

No destination broadcast via AIS — WIND SHIFT's AIS destination field was blank throughout. This does not confirm that no pre-arrival notification was filed through other channels — Fiji Customs port records would need to be checked to establish this definitively. The absence of an AIS broadcast was the primary observable indicator of concern.

Wallis and Futuna origin — Sigave is the main port of a French collectivity with limited outbound customs screening. Vessels departing here do not face the same level of departure inspection as those leaving a major regulated port.

Northern EEZ entry point — Entry through Fiji's least-monitored corridor, outside primary Fiji Navy patrol coverage.

48-hour transit, no broadcast destination — Two days at sea from Wallis and Futuna with no declared port of call on AIS represented the core customs compliance concern.

Overall risk assessment: MEDIUM-HIGH. Intercept recommended.

Phase 5 — Report and Customs Notification

NA.YADRA generated formal EEZ Incursion Alert Ref: NA-EEZ-20260701-001 for Fiji Customs and Fiji Navy, including full vessel particulars, chronological situation summary, assessed risk indicators with appropriate caveats, and recommended actions for Fiji Customs, Fiji Navy Northern Command, and Savusavu Customs Post.

Critically, the absence of a pre-arrival notification was reported as unconfirmed — flagged for FRCS records verification rather than stated as established fact. Intelligence that overstates certainty erodes the trust of the agencies that rely on it. The report was transmitted to Fiji Customs with the formal alert PDF attached.

"Intelligence that knows its own limits is more useful, not less, than intelligence that does not."

Operational Timeline

00:00 — Initial query, no DB result. 00:05 — Bounding box audit, coverage gap identified. 00:10 — Box expanded to full northern corridor. 00:15 — VesselFinder acquisition, vessel confirmed inside EEZ. 00:20 — WIND SHIFT seeded into vessels.db. 00:22 — EEZ monitor alert fired, UNACKNOWLEDGED. 00:23 — /api/eez/alerts confirmed live. 00:30 — Report NA-EEZ-20260701-001 generated. 00:35 — Report transmitted to Fiji Customs.

What This Operation Demonstrates

Coverage is intelligence. Identifying the northern EEZ gap has permanently improved NA.YADRA's domain awareness across the corridor carrying the greatest unmonitored risk.

Speed matters. From initial query to formal report transmission, the entire operation was conducted within a single analytical session — with 12–18 hours remaining before estimated landfall.

Caveats are not weakness. Analytical honesty about what AIS data can and cannot confirm builds the long-term trust of the agencies that act on intelligence.

The EEZ monitor works. Within one 60-second monitoring cycle of being seeded into the database, WIND SHIFT was automatically flagged, alerted, and served to the platform API.

Status: ACTIVE MONITORING — vessel inside Fiji EEZ, tracking toward Vanua Levu. Case open.
